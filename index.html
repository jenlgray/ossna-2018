What are the Problems?

Steve: Late in 2016, we started talking to devs at Adobe to learn what the problems are with us not contributing back to OS.

Confusing process to contribute to OS

  • Email based process
  • Composed of Legal, Compliance, Patent, Branding
  • Took months to complete
Steve:
- Emails were easily lost in inboxes.
- Each team ran their own process, documented seperately from each other.
- Very long time

Employees avoided or ignored the process

  • Gave up on existing OS submissions
  • Legal became the "boogyman"
  • Started pushing to GitHub without approval (Big Risk)
Steve:
- gave up or avoided the existing process
- teams started avoiding legal. they did everything they could to not initiate contact
- so the end result was pushing into the public domain without approval - BIG RISK if you talk to your legal friends!

Wild West on GitHub

  • 35 Adobe related GitHub Orgs
  • Repos abandoned, issues and PRs ignored
Steve:
- there was no central solution
- the easiest way forward was for teams to create and manage their own org. We ended up with 35 Adobe related GitHub orgs.
- With no commitment to continue to support, many of those 35 orgs were abondanded without any notice. Sad to see issues and pull requests ignored for years. No central authority to provide guidelines around archiving these repos.

Adobe's Open Source Office

Steve:
- To deal with these problems, we formed the OSO at the end of 2016.
- It was a grass roots type effort, with us only working on it part time.
- The original members were essentially volunteers as they had other responsibilities as part of their day jobs.
- The thing that brought them together was a shared love of open source and their desire to have Adobe give back to the community.

Grow Open Source at Adobe

Steve: The OSO had one clear goal, that was to grow open source at Adobe. Help teams open source existing projects and contribute back to community OS projects.
Starter repo
Steve:
- One of the first things we did was create a starter-repo.
- This contained the necessesary files for open source projects such as:
- a readme file describing the basics
- a contributing.md file to help newcomers get involved
- Code of conduct based on the popular Contributor Covenant code of conduct.
- a license file was mandatory (apache 2 or MIT)
- and optional issue and pull request templates

Revamped OS Submission Process

Steve: The next obvious thing to tackle was the submission process! So we started to gather all of the requirements from all of the various teams involved in the OS process. We took those requirements and created a checklist for teams to follow.
Steve:
- To manage the process, we created a git repo on our internal github. We put the checklist of all of the requirements into a issue_template.
- Every submitter was required to create an issue in our repo and start filling out the checklist in the issue template.
- One big benefit of this new process meant that it was open and transparent. Anyone could view previous submissions and see how they went. We wanted to remove the mystery behind the approvals.
- Over time and many discussions with various stakeholders, the list grew to 53 items. The process was still daunting and a little confusing.
- Our plan was for the checklist to be a first pass. It was quick to get up and it allowed us to build relationships with the stakeholders. It gave us at the open source office time to learn the nuances of contributing to and open sourcing code.
- So earlier this year, from our learnings, we launched the new open source submission form. (Hand it off to Fil to talk about that)
Fil: The new submission form is radically simpler.
- We decided to remove the ambiguous questions for more simple and direct ones - easier to understand for developers (i.e. not written by lawyers).
- We created categorizes of open source projects that the open source office would be able to approve without going through all of the stakeholders.
- It bolted on top of the existing process - Users logged in with their enterprise Git account, and issues were submitted to the existing issue tracker. No surprises for anynone already involved, just a nice UI on top of existing process.
- Shout out to Joni to helping us make the experience much better.

New Approvelist and Denylist

Fil: We also created a approvelist of previously approved projects as well as a denylist of projects that were out of bounds.
- This means if one employee received approval for contributing to a project, all other Adobe employees could do so as well without needing to fill out forms or ask for permission.
- We integratated these lists right into our submission form to give instant approvals or denials. This significantly sped up our approval times.

Open Development Template
Fil: The intent is to answer the questions: “how can I create a project that is open to newcomers, easy to involve yourself in, and scales as it grows in size?” Main areas, with a focus on answering why and how to document:
- Mission: what are the goals of the project?
- Licensing: what is it, what kinds exist out there?
- Governance: how do we add people to the project? Who has permissions in the project? What are the social rituals in the project?
- Process: how does the project get work done?
Developed internally to help teams grow cross-organizational projects that involve people from all across the company. It is a grass-roots effort to subvert the typical corporate hierarchical organization form and its limitations.
- We are dogfooding this process within the office, in order to make it more open and inviting for anyone to jump in and help out. We've made a few things about the way we work explicit, such as:
- 2 week sprints - everything in the open - self-assigned issues - accountability around what's assigned each sprint meeting

OSS-Contributors project

  • Scan GitHub every month to get company-to-user associations as reported by people's Company field on their GitHub profile
  • We cross-reference that with commit activity on GitHub
  • Original Idea from a blog post by Felipe Hoffa, Developer Advocate at Google
Fil: we have yet to open source this project, but it's an attempt at quantifying our ranking among other companies.
- every month we pound the github REST API and keep track of user to company associations in our own DB
- we cross reference this information with commit activity on GitHub
- we stole Felipe Hoffa's approach from his blog post on this topic, and expanded on it a bit
- there are many flaws of this approach: we only count commits as contributions (whereas we want to count comments, PRs, reviews, etc.). the company-to-user associations are rough at best. but that's ok.
